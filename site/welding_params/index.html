    Welding Technique¶

    Basic Principles¶

    1. Arc Length¶

    CRITICALLY IMPORTANT: Maintain the shortest possible arc!

    • Optimal: 1-2 mm (for 2-2.5 mm electrodes)
    • Maximum: 3 mm (for 3 mm electrode)
    • Too short: sticking risk, but easier to avoid with HF ignition
    • Too long: unstable arc, excessive spatter, lack of penetration

    Visual control: - Correct arc: bright spot, clear boundaries - Too long: blurred spot, crackling, sparks - Too short: electrode "bogs down" in puddle

    2. Electrode Angle¶

    Flat position: - Angle to surface: 60-70° - Tilt: backward from welding direction by 10-20° - Transverse motion: minimal or none (for thin metal)

    Vertical position: - Upward: 70-80° angle to surface - Downward: not recommended for thin metal - Short arcs, pulses

    Horizontal position: - Angle: 70-80° to vertical wall - Slightly downward to prevent sagging

    Overhead position: - Avoid if possible - Angle: 80-90° to surface - Only short pulses - Minimum current

    3. Welding Speed¶

    General rule: - Thinner metal → faster welding - Watch the weld pool - Uniform speed = uniform bead

    Signs of correct speed: - Pool moves evenly behind electrode - Constant bead width - No burn-through or lack of penetration

    Too fast: - Narrow convex bead - Lack of penetration - Edge fusion failure

    Too slow: - Wide flat bead - Burn-through risk - Metal overheating

    Intermittent Technique for Thin Metal¶

    This is the key technique for 1-1.5 mm thick metal!

    Operating Principle¶

    1. Arc Ignition
    2. Bring electrode to 3-5 mm
    3. Activate HF ignition

    4. Immediately close to 1-2 mm

    5. Working Pulse

    6. Duration: 0.5-1.5 seconds
    7. Form small weld pool

    8. Advance 5-10 mm

    9. Arc Break

    10. Quickly pull electrode back 5-7 mm
    11. Arc extinguishes

    12. Metal begins cooling

    13. Pause

    14. Duration: 0.5-2 seconds
    15. Observe pool solidification

    16. Prepare for next pulse

    17. Re-ignition

    18. Bring electrode to solidified pool
    19. HF ignition penetrates vitrified coating
    20. Continue the weld

    Pulse Parameters¶

    For 1.0 mm thickness: - Pulse duration: 0.5-1.0 sec - Pause: 1.0-1.5 sec - Travel: 3-5 mm per pulse

    For 1.5 mm thickness: - Pulse duration: 1.0-1.5 sec - Pause: 0.5-1.0 sec - Travel: 5-8 mm per pulse

    For 2.0 mm thickness: - Pulse duration: 1.5-2.0 sec - Pause: 0.3-0.5 sec (or continuous) - Travel: 8-12 mm per pulse

    Working Rhythm¶

    Important: Develop a stable rhythm!

    Example: "One-two (pulse) - pause - One-two (pulse) - pause"

    Uniform rhythm ensures: - Equal heat input along entire weld - Predictable quality - No overheating

    Continuous Welding¶

    For metal from 1.5 mm, continuous welding without breaks is possible.

    Technique: 1. Ignite arc 2. Maintain stable short arc (1-2 mm) 3. Move uniformly along joint 4. Minimal or no transverse motion 5. Watch the weld pool

    Advantages: - Higher productivity - Continuous bead - Less vitrified coating inclusion in weld

    Disadvantages: - More heat input - Distortion risk - Requires higher skill level

    Modes for Specific Tasks¶

    Tack Welds¶

    Purpose: Fix parts before final welding

    Parameters: - Current: 20-30% below working current - Tack length: 5-10 mm - Distance between tacks: 20-30 mm - Technique: single short pulses

    Sequence: 1. Ignite arc 2. Short pulse 0.5-1 sec 3. Form small fusion point 4. Break arc 5. Move to next point

    HF Ignition Features: - No need to break vitrified coating - Fast movement between points - High productivity

    Spot Welding¶

    Purpose: Join parts at discrete points without continuous bead

    Parameters: - Current: working or +10-15% - Duration: 1-2 seconds - Spot diameter: 4-8 mm - Distance between spots: per design requirements

    Technique: 1. HF ignition 2. Pulse with penetration formation 3. Arc break 4. Move to next point

    Welding Closed Contours¶

    Problem: Metal is cold at start of weld, overheated at end

    Solution:

    1. Weld Start:
    2. Begin with current 10% above working

    3. First 10-15 mm - metal preheating

    4. Middle Section:

    5. Working current

    6. Stable mode

    7. Weld End (tie-in):

    8. 20-30 mm before start, reduce current by 10-20%
    9. Tie in weld with 5-10 mm overlap
    10. Gradually reduce current (if Down Slope function available)

    Welding Dissimilar Thicknesses¶

    Example: 1.0 mm + 2.0 mm

    Principle: More heat to thick part, less to thin

    Technique: 1. Current: average between values for each thickness 2. Electrode angle: directed predominantly toward thick part (60-70° to it) 3. Arc: more often on thick part, periodic touches to thin 4. Speed: medium, control penetration of thin part

    Example mode: - Thicknesses: 1.0 + 2.0 mm - Electrode: 2.5 mm - Current: 45-50 A (between 40 for 1mm and 50-60 for 2mm) - Technique: intermittent, arc 70% time on thick, 30% on thin

    Quality Control During Welding¶

    Visual Control¶

    Observe:

    1. Arc Color
    2. Bright white center → normal
    3. Red, dark → insufficient current or long arc

    4. Too bright, blinding → excessive current

    5. Arc Sound

    6. Smooth hiss → normal
    7. Crackling, clicks → unstable arc, check V-A curve and arc length

    8. Humming → possible magnetic blow, relocate ground

    9. Weld Pool

    10. Uniform, mobile → normal
    11. Too large, sagging → reduce current or increase speed

    12. Small, solidifying quickly → increase current or slow down

    13. Bead Formation

    14. Uniform ripples → normal
    15. Convex narrow bead → increase current or slow down
    16. Wide flat → reduce current or speed up
    17. Burn-through → reduce current, increase speed, use intermittent

    Heat Input Control¶

    Signs of overheating: - Metal begins glowing around weld - Distortion, warping - Burn-through - Weld pool too fluid

    Actions for overheating: 1. Reduce current by 5-10 A 2. Increase welding speed 3. Increase pauses between pulses 4. Allow part to cool 5. Use copper backing for heat dissipation

    Signs of insufficient heating: - Lack of penetration - Edge fusion failure - Convex weld - Slag removes poorly

    Actions: 1. Increase current by 5-10 A 2. Reduce welding speed 3. Shorten pauses between pulses 4. Improve edge preparation

    Parameter Adjustment During Work¶

    Problem and Solution Table (Quick Reference)¶

    Problem Possible Cause Solution
    Arc won't ignite HF not working Check settings, enable HF Start
    Sticking V-A curve too stiff Increase V/A coefficient to 1.5-2.0
    Excessive spatter Arc too long Reduce arc length to 1-2 mm
    Lack of penetration Insufficient current Increase current by 10-15 A
    Burn-through Excessive current Reduce current, use intermittent
    Arc deflects Magnetic blow Relocate ground
    Uneven bead Unstable speed Develop uniform rhythm
    Slag removes poorly Low current or wet electrodes Increase current, use dry electrodes

    Adaptation to Conditions¶

    When welding outdoors: - Wind protection mandatory (screens) - Wind cools metal → increase current by 10% - More spatter from wind → use protective screens

    At low air temperature: - Metal colder → increase current by 10-15% - Increase pulse duration - Preheat weld areas

    At high humidity: - Mandatory use of baked electrodes - Store in electrode oven - Increased porosity probability
